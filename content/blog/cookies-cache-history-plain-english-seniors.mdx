Every web browser — Chrome, Safari, Edge, Firefox — keeps three different kinds of notes about you as you use the internet. They get lumped together as &quot;browser data&quot; and half the pop-ups you see ask you to clear them. But they do very different jobs, and clearing the wrong one can be a minor inconvenience.

Here is each one, in plain English.

## Cookies — the website&apos;s memory about you

A cookie is a tiny text file that a website leaves on your computer, so it can remember you next time. They&apos;re genuinely named &quot;cookies&quot; — the name is 30 years old and nobody&apos;s changed it.

### What cookies do

- Keep you signed in (so you don&apos;t have to type your password every single visit)
- Remember what you put in your online shopping basket
- Remember your language and country settings
- Remember that you clicked &quot;no thanks&quot; to the newsletter popup (the reason websites keep asking when you&apos;re on a new device — the cookie isn&apos;t there yet)

### Two kinds of cookies

**First-party cookies** — left by the website you&apos;re actually visiting. These are usually useful. Without them, the website would forget you the moment you turned the page.

**Third-party cookies** — left by other companies (usually advertising networks) that the website embeds. These are the ones that follow you around. You search for a pair of shoes once, and then see shoe adverts on every website for a fortnight. That&apos;s third-party cookies at work.

The good news: most major browsers now block or restrict third-party cookies by default. Safari has done so for years. Chrome finally caught up in 2024.

### When to clear cookies

- When a website stops working right (&quot;logged out,&quot; &quot;session expired,&quot; odd errors). Clearing cookies for just that site often fixes it.
- When you&apos;ve finished using a shared or public computer.
- When you&apos;re selling or giving away a computer.
- Every few months for general hygiene, if you want to.

### When NOT to clear cookies

- If you do not want to re-enter passwords on every site you&apos;re signed into.
- Right before an online purchase (you&apos;ll lose your basket).
- If your browser is working fine.

## Cache — saved copies of web pages

When you visit a website, your browser quietly saves local copies of the images, stylesheets, and other content. Next time you visit the same site, your browser uses those copies instead of downloading them again. That&apos;s the cache (pronounced &quot;cash&quot;).

### What the cache does

It makes websites load faster — particularly sites you visit often. Amazon, your bank, Wikipedia, your local news — the second visit is always quicker than the first because so much is already cached on your computer.

### When to clear the cache

- When a website is behaving strangely — broken images, missing buttons, a page that loads halfway. The cached copy is outdated and clearing forces a fresh download.
- When a website has &quot;updated&quot; but you&apos;re still seeing the old version.
- Rarely — maybe twice a year for hygiene.

### A good analogy

The cache is like keeping a photocopy of a menu at home so you don&apos;t have to walk to the restaurant every time you want to look. Useful — until the restaurant changes the menu and you&apos;re still looking at the old one.

### When NOT to clear the cache

- Daily. It undermines the point. The cache is supposed to speed things up; clear it too often and you&apos;re downloading everything from scratch.

## History — the list of where you&apos;ve been

Your browser keeps a list of every website you&apos;ve visited, in order, often going back several months or years. This is **history**.

### What history does

- Lets you type part of a website name in the address bar and have the full address autocomplete
- Lets you go back and find &quot;that article I read last week about tulips&quot;
- Helps your browser guess what you&apos;re looking for

### When to clear history

- Shared or family computers where you&apos;d rather your search trail didn&apos;t live on.
- After using someone else&apos;s computer.
- Before selling or giving away a computer.
- Occasionally for general privacy, especially on work machines.

### What history is NOT

- It is not a record that websites keep of you. It&apos;s only the record your own browser keeps. Websites still know you visited from their own server logs, which clearing your history does not touch.
- It is not the same as Incognito mode. Incognito just means your browser doesn&apos;t save history for that window. For more on this distinction, see our [plain-English guide to VPNs, Incognito, and Private DNS](/blog/vpn-vs-incognito-vs-private-dns-plain-english).

## The three, side-by-side

| Name | What it is | Clear when |
|------|-----------|-----------|
| **Cookies** | Website&apos;s memory about you | Site is broken; sold/shared computer |
| **Cache** | Your copy of website images | Site looks wrong; occasionally for hygiene |
| **History** | Your list of sites visited | Sold/shared computer; occasional privacy |

## How to actually clear each

### In Chrome (Android, Windows, Mac)

1. Click the three-dot menu (top right)
2. **Settings** → **Privacy and security** → **Clear browsing data**
3. Tick the boxes for what you want cleared
4. Choose a time range (&quot;last hour,&quot; &quot;last 7 days,&quot; &quot;all time&quot;)
5. Click **Clear data**

### In Safari (iPhone, iPad)

1. **Settings** → **Safari**
2. Scroll down → **Clear History and Website Data** (this clears all three)
3. Or, for more control: **Settings** → **Safari** → **Advanced** → **Website Data** → remove individual sites

### In Safari (Mac)

1. **Safari menu** → **Clear History**
2. Choose a time range
3. Click **Clear History**

### In Edge (Windows)

1. Click the three-dot menu
2. **Settings** → **Privacy, search, and services** → **Clear browsing data** → **Choose what to clear**
3. Tick the boxes, choose a time range, click **Clear now**

### In Firefox

1. Click the three-line menu (top right)
2. **History** → **Clear recent history**
3. Tick the boxes, choose a time range, click **Clear Now**

## The &quot;clear everything nuclear option&quot;

If you want to hand your computer to someone else, or you think something has gone wrong, you can do all three at once. Most browsers have an &quot;All time&quot; option in the clear-browsing-data menu that wipes cookies, cache, and history from the beginning of time.

After doing this you will:
- Be logged out of every website
- Need to dismiss newsletter popups you&apos;ve previously dismissed
- See slightly slower loads on favourite sites for a day or two (cache rebuilding)
- Lose your autocomplete suggestions in the address bar

That&apos;s it. Nothing else is broken. This is worth knowing because it&apos;s a very safe, very thorough thing to do occasionally.

## What about those annoying cookie pop-ups?

Since 2018, websites in the UK and EU have been required to ask for permission to set certain cookies. Most seniors we talk to find these pop-ups exhausting. Two things to know:

- **&quot;Accept all&quot;** lets the website set every kind of cookie, including advertising ones. Convenient, less private.
- **&quot;Reject all&quot;** or **&quot;Essential only&quot;** lets the website set only the cookies it needs to work. Slightly less convenient, more private.

For most seniors we recommend &quot;Reject all&quot; or the essentials-only option when given the choice. You won&apos;t notice any difference in how the website works.

## A quick note on safety

None of cookies, cache, or history are dangerous on their own. They don&apos;t cause viruses, they can&apos;t &quot;infect&quot; your computer, and they don&apos;t make you vulnerable to scams. Anyone who tells you otherwise — usually via a pop-up screaming &quot;Your computer is at risk! Click here to clean it!&quot; — is running a scam. Check any suspicious alert through our [scam message checker](/tools/scam-checker) and check unfamiliar links with your usual caution.

## Bottom line

- **Cookies** = website&apos;s memory about you. Clear when a site breaks.
- **Cache** = saved images to speed things up. Clear if a site looks wrong.
- **History** = your own record of where you&apos;ve been. Clear when sharing a computer.

Three simple ideas. Clear them the right way, at the right time, for the right reason. Your browser will look after itself the rest of the time.
