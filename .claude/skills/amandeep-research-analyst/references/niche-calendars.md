# Niche Topical Calendars — Cricket & Senior Tech

Timing is a huge variable in content research. A topic that bombs in February can peak in April. Load this reference when the user's question has a timing component ("what should we post this week?", "plan for next month", etc.).

## 🏏 Cricket Content Calendar (cricjosh.in)

### The Annual Rhythm

Cricket content for Indian audiences runs on a predictable cycle dominated by tentpole events. Off-season is a fundamentally different content game — don't apply in-season playbooks to it.

**Peak windows (highest traffic, highest competition):**
- **IPL season** (~late March to late May) — the single biggest cricket content window of the year for Indian audiences
- **ICC major tournaments** (World Cups, Champions Trophy) — dates vary, but each pulls massive traffic for 3–6 weeks
- **India international tours** (home & away Tests/ODIs/T20Is) — rolling through the year

**Shoulder windows (moderate traffic, lower competition):**
- Domestic tournaments (Ranji Trophy, Syed Mushtaq Ali, Vijay Hazare)
- Non-India international cricket (Ashes, PSL, BBL) — smaller audience but less crowded
- WPL and women's international cricket — growing audience, much less competition

**Low-traffic windows (pivot to different content types):**
- Mid-year gaps between series
- Late December during non-India tour weeks

### What to publish when

**During a live India match:**
- **Reactive content only**: live reactions, moment breakdowns, real-time hot takes
- **Avoid**: deep dives, evergreen pieces, tangential opinion
- **Timing discipline**: publish within minutes of the moment, not hours — the window closes fast

**During IPL but no live match that day:**
- Player spotlights, team analysis, strategy breakdowns
- Fantasy cricket content (very hungry audience)
- Previews for the next day's match
- Light takes and humor

**Off-season (cricket calendar gap):**
- Evergreen explainers ("How to read a scorecard", "What is DRS and why does it fail")
- Historical deep dives ("The greatest X innings of the decade")
- Player career retrospectives — especially during retirements or transfers
- Controversial takes / "best of all time" debates (high engagement, evergreen SEO)
- "Where are they now" content on older players
- Format comparisons (Test vs T20, technique evolution)

### The cricket viral story lifecycle

A typical cricket moment follows this arc — know where you are in the cycle:

1. **T+0 (moment happens)**: Specific incident — a catch, duck, sledge, DRS decision. Lives on X first.
2. **T+1 hour**: Screenshots and clips spread across X and WhatsApp.
3. **T+3 hours**: Reddit threads on r/Cricket with significant upvotes.
4. **T+6 hours**: Short-form video platforms start producing clips.
5. **T+24 hours**: Analysis pieces and long-form YouTube videos.
6. **T+3 days**: Mainstream sports media caught up, "best angles" compilations out.
7. **T+1 week**: Meme evolution — remixed, referenced in unrelated contexts.
8. **T+1 month**: "Best moments of [tournament]" recap cycles bring it back.

**Opportunity windows for cricjosh.in:**
- **T+1 to T+3 hours**: Fastest-reactive text content on X and blog
- **T+6 to T+24 hours**: Explainer / analysis articles — the main cricjosh sweet spot
- **T+3 days to T+1 week**: "What we learned" synthesis pieces
- **T+1 month**: Recap and compilation formats

### Cricket hot buttons (high engagement)

Use these with craft, not laziness:
- Captaincy decisions — always divisive
- Selection controversies — evergreen in Indian cricket
- Player vs player comparisons (Kohli/Rohit, Kohli/Babar, etc.)
- Umpiring and DRS
- Retirement and succession narratives
- Statistical oddities ("first time since 1998", "no one has ever...")

### What to avoid (saturated or risky for cricjosh)

- Pre-match "predictions" that duplicate every other cricket site's
- Generic "Top 10" lists without a novel angle or methodology
- Ball-by-ball commentary (broadcasters own this)
- Pure cross-border nationalism as the main hook (alienates readers, brand-risky, short-term)

## 💻 Senior Tech Content Calendar (techfor60s.com)

### The Annual Rhythm

Less event-driven than cricket, but with strong recurring windows tied to platforms, seasons, and life events.

**Predictable annual spikes:**

- **January** — "New year, new device." People received tablets, phones, smart speakers over the holidays and need setup help. Heavy "getting started" traffic.
- **February–April** — **Tax season** (both US and Indian fiscal years touch this window). Big interest in tax-filing tech, digital documents, fraud protection, secure payment methods.
- **May–July** — Travel season (navigation apps, translation, international calling, accessibility tools for travel).
- **September–October** — iPhone and Windows major releases. Creates a 2–3 week wave of "what changed" content.
- **November** — Holiday shopping scams spike. Scam awareness peaks; high-urgency content window.
- **December** — "Which device should I buy my [parent/grandparent]?" gift-guide content peaks for family-member readers.

**Platform-update windows (reliable but unpredictable timing):**
- Major iOS update (annually, usually September) — a 2–3 week window for "what's new / what changed / how to do X"
- Major Android update (staggered by manufacturer)
- WhatsApp, Facebook, Gmail, Zoom — any major UI change creates a content window for 1–2 weeks
- Windows feature updates

### What to publish when

**Always evergreen (steady traffic year-round via SEO):**
- "How to" content for common tasks (video calling, sharing photos, backing up data)
- Scam awareness (always relevant, peaks around holidays and tax season)
- Accessibility settings (larger text, screen readers, voice control, high-contrast mode)
- Privacy settings walkthroughs
- Simplification content ("you don't need these 8 apps", "turn off this one setting")

**Timely (ride news cycles):**
- Post-platform-update explainers — within 48 hours of the update
- Responses to specific scams circulating right now (verify before publishing; don't amplify false claims)
- Coverage of new devices/services seniors are actively asking about (check Reddit and Quora for signals)

**Seasonal calendar:**
- **January**: "Getting started with [device you got for the holidays]"
- **February–April**: Tax-filing tech, online banking safety, document digitization
- **May–July**: Travel tech (boarding passes, roaming, translation, offline maps)
- **September–October**: Post-update explainers for iOS, WhatsApp, etc.
- **November**: Holiday shopping scams, safe online payment
- **December**: Gift guides for seniors, setting up new devices before they arrive

### The senior-tech content lifecycle

Critical difference from cricket: **senior-tech content doesn't spike — it compounds.**

Typical arc:
1. **T+0 to T+1 week**: Initial social share (if any), direct traffic from subscribers.
2. **T+1 week to T+1 month**: Family-to-family sharing via email, WhatsApp, Messenger. Traffic accumulates steadily.
3. **T+1 to T+6 months**: SEO kicks in for evergreen "how to" content. Sustained organic traffic.
4. **T+6 months onward**: Annually-refreshed pieces keep compounding — no sharp decay.

**Opportunity windows for techfor60s.com:**
- **First 48 hours** after a platform update — be among the first to explain it clearly
- **First 1–2 weeks** after a new scam goes public — ride awareness without being too early to verify
- **Annually recurring**: tax season (Feb–Apr), holiday scam window (Nov), gift-guide season (Dec), back-to-school-but-for-grandparents (Jan)

### Senior tech hot buttons (high engagement, audience-respectful)

- **Scam awareness** — especially "new scams targeting seniors" content
- **Privacy concerns and data control** — settings walkthroughs, app permissions
- **Simplification** — "turn off / delete / remove" content outperforms "add / install / enable"
- **Peace of mind** — end-of-life digital planning, password legacy, account inheritance
- **Independence** — enabling seniors to do things without asking family for help
- **Intergenerational content** — pieces that bridge parents and adult children

### What to avoid (for techfor60s specifically)

- **Any condescension** — this audience detects it instantly and leaves. Never write as though the reader is slow.
- **Shock-value hooks** — "Your phone is SPYING on you!" styles undercut the trust you need with this audience.
- **Fast-paced listicles** — the audience reads more slowly; clarity > pace.
- **Buried main points** — get to the answer within the first 2–3 paragraphs.
- **Jargon without gentle explanation** — the moment you say "the cloud" or "two-factor authentication" without defining it, you've lost a chunk of readers.
- **Fear-farming** — yes, fear-based hooks would perform short-term on this audience. Ethically off the table for this site.

## 🎯 Using These Calendars in Research

When the user's question has a timing component, Amandeep checks the calendar before recommending:

- **Is the query during a peak window?** Advice changes entirely between seasons.
- **Is there an upcoming tentpole event?** (Next IPL match, next iOS update, tax deadline) — plan content *before*, not *after*.
- **Is the user asking about off-season opportunity?** Different playbook from peak season.

**Example 1:**

*User (in April): "What should we post on cricjosh this week?"*

Factor in: mid-IPL season, likely multiple matches this week, reactive-plus-analysis is the dominant play. *Don't* recommend an evergreen deep-dive into Bradman's technique — save that for August.

**Example 2:**

*User (in October): "What should we plan for techfor60s this month?"*

Factor in: post-iOS-update window (verify exact dates), approaching holiday scam season, pre-gift-guide season. *Do* recommend iOS-change explainers and early scam-awareness content.

**Example 3:**

*User (in July): "Cricket content is slow, what do we do?"*

Factor in: likely a calendar gap. This is the time for retrospectives, debate pieces, format comparisons, and evergreen SEO-targeting articles that will cash in when the next tournament starts. *Don't* try to create artificial urgency around non-existent news — the audience will see through it.
